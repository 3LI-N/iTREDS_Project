I. Context
A. National context and rationale for IFAD engagement

National context
Political, economic and social context. The fourth largest economy in the
West African subregion, Senegal has maintained a good level of economic growth
since 2012, averaging 5.4 per cent at the end of 2017. Thanks to higher
investment in agriculture and infrastructure, this growth is projected to reach 7 per
cent in the period 2018-2020 and 10 per cent in 2021-2024. Efforts to redistribute
the fruits of this growth have had an effect on the living conditions of the
population. However, with a human development score putting the country at 164th
in the world in 2018, Senegal needs to improve the quality of that growth to make
it more inclusive.
No Poverty (Sustainable Development Goal 1 [SDG 1]), zero hunger (SDG
2). Senegal’s population, estimated in 2019 at 16.2 million (more than 50 per cent
of it in the 15-35 age group), is growing at an annual rate of 2.7 per cent. More
than half the population (56 per cent) lives in rural areas where poverty is more
acute, with an estimated incidence of 57.1 per cent compared with 47 per cent
nationally in 2011. Since 2016, the country’s good growth performance has
reduced this figure by 4 to 7 per cent; projections show an even faster decrease in
the poverty rate, which is expected to fall to 31 per cent by 2020 due to
agricultural growth.
Policies and strategies for family farming, poverty reduction and increased
food security. The Emerging Senegal Plan (PSE) 2035 establishes the basis and
main orientations for realizing the country’s development vision. These orientations
have been developed in sector strategies, among them: (i) the Senegal Agriculture
Acceleration Programme (PRACAS);1 (ii) the National Employment Policy
(2017-2020);2 (iii) the vocational and technical education law.
Special aspects relating to IFAD’s corporate mainstreaming priorities
Senegal needs to improve the quality of its economic growth to make it more
inclusive and capable of contributing to the improvement of social indicators, since:
(i) the incidence of rural poverty is high (57 per cent) and affects the food security
and resilience of farm families and low-income households; (ii) youth employment
is low (38 per cent), and new waves of young people (160,000) are entering the
job market every year; (iii) rural underemployment is particularly high
(31 per cent) and contributes to legitimizing the phenomenon of migration and
rural exodus in the collective consciousness.
5. Within this context, the Rural Youth Agripreneur Support Project (Agrijeunes Tekki
Ndawñi) is positioned as a rural transformation initiative targeting rural youth
excluded from the wealth creation process due to a lack of productive and
innovative jobs in the agro-sylvo-pastoral and fishery sector. Its aim is to enable
them to become agricultural entrepreneurs.
In terms of food security, Senegal ranks 66th out of 119 countries on the Global
Hunger Index (GHI, 2018). The results of the rural agriculture survey indicate a
need for particular attention to the Casamance region where the project will be
implemented.

Gender and social inclusion.3 The Gender Inequality Index (GII)4 ranked
Senegal 124th out of 160 countries in 2017. The barriers to women’s participation
in the production system are associated with unequal access to means of
production, little representation of young women in decision-making bodies and
limited skills. The second phase of the National Strategy for Gender Equality and
Equity (SNEEG II 2016-2026) and the gender equality and women's empowerment
strategy of the country strategic opportunities programme (COSOP) will help lift
these barriers.
Persons with disabilities.5 In the project area, the Ziguinchor region has the
highest prevalence (8.1 per cent), while the Diourbel and Kaolack regions have the
lowest (5 per cent). The most widespread type of disability is mobility impairment
(1.5 per cent), which is an impediment to participation in the workforce. Agrijeunes
Tekki Ndawñi will facilitate the social and occupational inclusion of persons with
disabilities through collaboration with entities with expertise in this field.
Climate change.6 Farmland, soil and certain ecosystems are suffering from the
effects of climate variability. The combined effect of lower precipitation and higher
temperatures has led to a loss of vegetation cover, wind and water erosion, the
degradation of denuded soils and the salinization of land southward from the North.
Rationale for IFAD engagement
10. To improve living conditions, the vast majority of Senegalese households
(37 per cent), particularly in rural areas, consider youth employment as the top
investment priority.7
11. As in other countries in the subregion, the problem of rural youth employment in
Senegal has repercussions for securing a decent income. This issue is at the heart
of IFAD’s mandate for youth, and the Fund’s experiences8 in Africa through its
projects9 reveal that an effective response lies in building an economic model that
addresses the insecurity and underemployment that drive young people towards
exodus and migration.
12. The Government’s objective is to improve their employment prospects by creating
100,000-150,000 decent jobs each year through the promotion of investment in
sectors in the agro-sylvo-pastoral and fishery value chains with high employment
potential.
13. However, to seize these opportunities and tap into the aspirations of rural youth,
the systematic adoption of an integrated approach will be necessary. This approach
will entail: (i) turning family farms into modern, market-oriented enterprises;
(ii) promoting rural youth entrepreneurship; (iii) providing certified vocational
training; (iv) creating a reserve of remunerative jobs for youth; (v) lifting the
barriers that limit access to means of production such as land, information, the
market, financing and services.
14. The project will adopt this approach, which IFAD has successfully used in Africa,
turning family farms into family farming enterprises, and producers into
agripreneurs involved in all aspects of the value chain.

B. Lessons learned from experience
15. Young people are the priority target group. Following PAFA, the IFAD-funded
PADAER and PAFA-E projects have confirmed that support for rural youth
necessarily went hand in hand with a holistic intervention approach based on an in-
depth study and multi-context analysis of opportunities in the agriculture sector
and young people's aspirations.
16.
IFAD’s experiences in Africa10 reveal that the guarantee of a decent income is
essential for motivating youth to launch business projects in the agro-sylvo-
pastoral and fishery sector.
17. Drawing lessons from interventions around the world to promote rural youth
employment, the Yaoundé Declaration of 1 March 201911 stressed the importance
of: (i) rejuvenating the farming community by attracting young people to
agriculture; (ii) building capacity to boost agropastoral entrepreneurship among
youth; (iii) promoting innovation and intensifying the use of information and
communications technologies. The Agrijeunes Tekki Ndawñi project embraces this
approach, is informed by past experiences and will contribute to the creation of
knowledge and skills. Furthermore, this knowledge will be capitalized on through
IFAD’s regional grant for the development of tools aimed at attracting youth to
agriculture and the agrifood sector (2018-2021).12
II. Project description
A. Objectives, geographic area of intervention and target groups
Pursuant to the national strategic orientations for agricultural development, the
18.
project is part of the COSOP 2019-2024 and will contribute to the achievement of
the SDGs on reducing poverty (SDG 1), eliminating hunger (SDG 2), promoting
gender equality (SDG 5), promoting decent work and economic growth (SFG 8)
and fighting climate change (SDG 13).
19. Development objective and impact. The project’s objective is to promote the
social and occupational inclusion of youth in family farms and profitable ventures
that create income and decent sustainable jobs13 in agro-sylvo-pastoral and fishery
value chains. The project will benefit 150,000 rural young people. Among this
group, 45,000 young people (50 per cent of whom will be female) will be
integrated into or receive support to start up a sustainable profitable activity in the
agro-sylvo-pastoral and fishery value chains. At project completion, 25,000 viable
enterprises14 in the agro-sylvo-pastoral and fishery value chains will have been
created and/or strengthened and will create 35,000 decent jobs, 50 per cent of
which will be to the benefit of young women. Furthermore, through capacity-
building, the project will have qualitative implications for the country’s nutrition
situation, which is already the best in West Africa.
20. The project will be implemented in four agroecological zones in Senegal: the Niayes
region, the groundnut basin, the sylvopastoral zone and lower and middle
Casamance. The project target group consists of: (i) youth aged 15-3515 seeking
economic inclusion; and (ii) economically active young entrepreneurs. The project’s
targeting strategy is intended to be inclusive and to promote the economic and
occupational integration of the poorest and most marginalized groups, such as

B. Components/results and activities
21. The project will have the following components: (i) development of profitable
economic activities; (ii) capacity-building and facilitation of inclusion; and (iii)
coordination and management.
22. Component 1: Development of profitable economic activities. The expected
outcome of this component is that rural youth will be engaged in remunerative
economic activities that guarantee them a decent income in the agro-sylvo-
pastoral and fishery value chains. The project will contribute to the market
inclusion of rural youth and their access to means of production by providing
start-up capital to youth in training or a productive credit to those who wish to
develop an existing activity. These young people will receive support that will aid
them in developing partnerships with farmers’ organizations, umbrella
organizations, interprofessional associations and other private sector operators.
23. Component 2: Capacity-building and facilitation of inclusion. The expected
outcome is that youth will acquire the necessary capacity to sustainably manage
their activities. To accomplish this, the mechanism for assisting rural youth will be
based on their profiles and needs. The project will study local youth mobilization
organizations, identify interested rural youth through a rural vocational project and
assist them in the preparation and implementation of microprojects and business
plans. It will also improve the quality and diversity of training, contributing to the
development of a sustainable training supply.
24. Component 3: Coordination, administrative and financial management and
monitoring and evaluation. This component will ensure the project’s
administrative and fiduciary management by focusing on results and transparency,
coordinate fieldwork with the technical team and supporting partners and will
organize the monitoring and evaluation system and gender-sensitive knowledge
management and communications systems. Finally, it will link project activities
with national initiatives, publish the data and create a dedicated website, thereby
strengthening national accountability mechanisms. It will also set up a third-party
monitoring mechanism.
C. Theory of change
25. The project is positioned as a rural transformation initiative aimed at strengthening
the agricultural initiatives of rural youth to enable them to become modern
agripreneurs benefiting from a viable and sustainable income.
26.
It seeks to (i) eliminate the access barriers to information and youth advisory and
counselling services; (ii) boost the limited skills and the technical and managerial
knowledge of youth and (iii) reduce barriers to financial services for youth, which,
along with land tenure issues, hinder their engagement and thwart their ambitions.
27. These problems: (i) spur migration and rural exodus, leading to the depopulation
of rural areas; (ii) dampen the spirit of enterprise; and (iii) increase
underemployment and unemployment.
28. To counteract these harmful effects, the project will adopt an entrepreneurial
transition approach that will promote: (i) sustainable engagement in attractive and
profitable rural occupations; (ii) business partnerships for production and
marketing at remunerative prices; (iii) technical and entrepreneurial capacity-
building; (iv) business relationships with financial and information and
communications technology institutions to build networks and connect young
agripreneurs to the broader economy.

D. Alignment, ownership and partnerships
30. The Agrijeunes Tekki Ndawñi project will contribute to the achievement of the
aforementioned SDGs. It is also aligned with the PSE and IFAD’s Strategic
Framework.
31. Ownership. Alignment with national orientations makes the project a priority of
the Government, which will play an integral role in implementation at the different
levels (central, deconcentrated and decentralized) through supervision, technical
services and local groups. Ownership will be facilitated by these groups’
participation in planning bodies, the execution of certain activities (supported by
agreements) and monitoring. The sector monitoring system will record the
project’s achievements in terms of their contribution to meeting PSE priorities.
32. Partnerships. The project will be implemented within the existing policy dialogue
33.
framework to ensure that it complies with current strategic directions and
practices, and is run in synergy and complementarity with the operations of other
partners. It will therefore participate in joint planning sessions in the regions. It will
rely on PADAER’s and PAFA-E’s knowledge of the area to build institutional and
performance partnerships for outsourcing needs. Service partnerships will be
subject to periodic evaluations in the interests of the project and its beneficiaries to
ensure that problems are properly communicated and analysed and solutions are
identified in a participatory manner.
In line with COSOP strategic objective 3 (engagement in national policy processes),
IFAD and its subregional office will make use of project outcomes by scaling them
up and integrating project methodologies into policies support the country’s youth.
Thus, partnerships for policy dialogue are defined by the COSOP, which provides
the framework for identifying the project's priority areas and actions. IFAD’s West
Africa Hub will increase cooperation with the other Rome-based United Nations
agencies (the Food and Agricultural Organization of the United Nations [FAO] and
the World Food Programme [WFP]) and the United Nations Development Assistance
Framework to promote more coherent and effective policy dialogue. Furthermore,
the regional hub will cooperate with the African Development Bank (AfDB) and the
World Bank, as needed.

Flow of funds will be managed through a designated account opened in a leading

Summary benefits and economic analysis
41. The project’s economic internal rate of return is 23.4 per cent. This result linked to
the increase in agripreneur income is highly satisfactory and buttressed with social
benefits: nutrition, well-being, climate resilience, the effects of capacity-building,
etc. In addition, the sensitivity analysis indicates that the results obtained are
highly robust.
Exit strategy and sustainability
42. The project’s sustainability will be ensured through: (i) the inclusion of rural youth
in the value chain dynamic and sustainable interaction with outside actors;
7
EB 2019/127/R.34/Rev.1
(ii) facilitation of access to land and secure land tenure, the dissemination of
innovations and support for the adoption of effective techniques and specialized
advisory support; (iii) assistance to youth over several production cycles to
facilitate their sustainable integration into the local and national economic context
and to lower their risk of failure; (iv) development of a sustainable diversified
supply of quality vocational training to meet the needs of rural youth;
(v) collaboration with professional organizations to mobilize expertise and youth
advisory services; and (vi) the forging of partnerships with deconcentrated
technical entities, local groups and delegated project management agencies.
III. Risks
A. Project risks and mitigations measures
43. The most likely risks are linked to the implementation environment, notably the
possibility that the project could be confused with other “youth” programmes or
initiatives with different approaches and methods. These risks will be mitigated with
the rapid launch of a major information and communication campaign, the adoption
of an inclusive targeting approach, the development of a joint approach to attract
other centres of interest to youth, as well as the dissemination of innovations that
will enable youth to distinguish themselves from competitors. Regular
communication with the technical services will smooth out any problems that arise
in their activities and will also help mitigate risk.
44. The integrated risk framework in appendix II indicates the other risks and their
mitigation measures.
B. Environmental and social category
45. The project will be exposed to a series of risks linked to the sectoral and
institutional reform context, and then to external factors such as fluctuations in
international markets and climate risk. These risks have been identified and
mitigation measures put in place. The project is classified in category B from the
environmental and social standpoint, and its environmental and social impact
should be less negative.
C. Climate risk classification
46. The project is categorized as having “high” climate risk. The technologies promoted
will be climate-resilient with respect to both the variability of rainfall, which will
require better water conservation and irrigation techniques, and the desertification
of the northern regions, which will involve a greater need to protect and safeguard
the agripreneurs' agricultural investments.
D. Debt sustainability
47. Senegal's economic growth rate has been over 6 per cent for the past three years,
and the medium-term economic outlook is favourable. Despite this sustained
growth, however, the debt-to-GDP ratio continues to increase, and the deterioration
in the terms of trade has led to a rise in the budget and current account deficits.
48.
In order to manage the high debt service level, the debt management strategy
should emphasize constant recourse to concessional loans. (Source: IMF).

A. Organizational framework
Project management and coordination
49. The project will be under the Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Equipment (MAER)
and presided over by the national steering committee (NSC). The NSC will create a
coordination and management unit to ensure that the project follows results-based
management principles, supported by operational mechanisms and adherence to
administrative, financial and procurement procedures.

Implementation will rely on: (i) institutional partnerships; (ii) the delegation of

technical operators and specialized implementing agencies; training, technical
assistance and advisory entities; local non-governmental organizations;
(iii) strategic partnerships with professional organizations for rural youth,
interprofessional associations, the Chamber of Trades, the Chamber of Commerce,
financial institutions, etc.
Financial management, procurement and governance
In fiduciary management, the overall project risk is “high” in the design stage
but will become “moderate” with mitigation measures. Analysis of the project’s
financial management mechanism shows that the recommended architecture meets
the basic requirements for the traceability of financial information (acquisition of
accounting software, quality management tools and experienced personnel).
52. Procurement will be governed by the code and procedures in force in Senegal,
which are acceptable and consistent with IFAD principles in this regard. A
procurement plan based on the annual workplan and budget will be prepared each
year. This plan will specify procurement methods, estimated costs, the timetable,
etc.
53. Governance. Corruption in the public sector remains a problem. With a score of 45
on Transparency International’s Corruption Perceptions Index in 2018 and 2017, the
country has stagnated in this regard, dropping from 66th to 67th place out of 180
countries in 2018.
54. The project will apply the principles of good governance and transparency:
(i) through the supervision of farmers’/producers’ organizations and rural youth for
implementation of the production partnership business plans; (ii) through the
monitoring of the compliance of farmers’ organizations with their internal by-laws
and regulations, particularly in the matter of filling positions in elected bodies; and
finally (iii) strict compliance with financial management and procurement
procedures.
B. Planning, monitoring and evaluation, learning, knowledge
management and communication
55. The monitoring and evaluation system will be aligned with the results-based
management system. It will measure progress in implementation and review
indicators according to the Operational Results Management System developed by
IFAD. The monitoring and evaluation system will be strengthened through PRiME.
Knowledge management and communication will be guided by a management plan
prepared at project start-up.
Innovations and scaling up
C.
56. The project is innovative in: (i) its overall response strategy of increasing income
and improving the resilience of rural youth; (ii) the promotion of sustainable
business models tailored to the situation of youth; (iii) its inclusive approach, which
heightens the integration, leadership and civic engagement of rural youth in
professional organizations and the economic and social development process.
Certain innovations drawn from positive experiences in Africa will be scaled up,
notably the development of effective partnerships with the myriad youth projects in
Senegal and support for public authorities to set up a national programme for rural
youth.
57.

Implementation plan. In formulating the project, the legal requirements that
often delay the start of activities were anticipated with the provision of start-up
funds in the amount of CFA 300 million. This will enable the project to meet the
requirements for the first disbursement, enabling it to maximize the time for the

Zone d'intervention du Projet. Le Projet interviendra dans 4 zones agro écologiques

et  moyenne  Casamance  dans  lesquelles  il  développera  des  partenariats  avec  des

formation  de  base,  exerçant  une  activité  économique  agro-sylvopastorale  et  halieutique

orphelins, handicapés, etc. vivant dans des familles ou centres d’accueil spécifiques.
3.
Finalité. Le Projet vise à renforcer les initiatives agricoles des jeunes ruraux exclus
des  processus  de  création  de  richesse,  faute  d’emplois  productifs  et  innovants  dans  le
secteur agro-sylvopastoral et halieutique, pour en faire des agripreneurs bénéficiant d'un
revenu viable et durable.

d’emplois  décents  et  durables  dans  les  chaines  de  valeur  agro-sylvopastorales  et
