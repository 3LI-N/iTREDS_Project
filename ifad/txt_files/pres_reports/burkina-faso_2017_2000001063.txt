Recommendation for approval
The Executive Board is invited to approve the recommendation on the proposed
financing for Burkina Faso for the Agricultural Value Chains Promotion Project, as
contained in paragraph 55.
Proposed loan and grant to Burkina Faso for the
Agricultural Value Chains Promotion Project (PAPFA)
I. Strategic context and rationale
A. Country and rural development and poverty context
1.
One of the main challenges facing the Government of Burkina Faso for the coming
decade continues to be achieving a structural transformation of the national
economy through strong, sustainable and inclusive growth underpinned largely by a
modernized agriculture sector. Between 2011 and 2016 Burkina Faso posted
substantial economic growth at an annual average rate of real GDP growth of 5.5
per cent – albeit below the 10.8 per cent target set in the country’s growth
strategies, in part as a result of poor agricultural performance. In order to reach its
goals, the Government recently adopted the National Economic and Social
Development Plan 2016-2020 (PNDES). The Plan sets forth guidelines for action
around three pillars, including promoting sectors with economic growth and job
creation potential, to shape a modern economy based on a more competitive
primary sector and more dynamic processing and services industries. This will
enable the country to achieve annual growth of 7.7 per cent and create at least
50,000 decent jobs per year.
2.
Burkina Faso has not yet updated its rural development policies. The rural
development strategy to 2015 prepared in 2004, and the National Rural Sector
Programme (PNSR) formulated in 2011, are currently under review based on the
guidelines set forth in the PNDES. The PNDES roadmap for transformation calls for
reaching the following targets by 2020: (i) a 50 per cent increase in primary sector
productivity and agricultural product marketing; (ii) increases in the volume of
processed agricultural products of 12 per cent to 25 per cent; and (iii) annual
average increases in production of 6.4 per cent for cereals and 7.6 per cent for cash
crops, based on emerging crops such as sesame.
B. Rationale and alignment with government priorities and RB-
3.
4.
COSOP
By developing four agricultural value chains – rice, market gardening, sesame and
cowpea – PAPFA will contribute to achieving the strategic objectives of PNDES
2016-2020. In particular, it will improve agricultural productivity, add more value to
agricultural products and promote entrepreneurship to overcome slow growth in
rural areas.
The strategic objectives of PNDES and PAPFA are aligned with IFAD’s Strategic
Framework 2016-2025. IFAD’s interventions in Burkina Faso are guided by the
country strategic opportunities programme (COSOP) dating from 2007. Strategic
objective 1 of the COSOP remains valid and aligned with those of the PNSR (2011-
2015) and PNDES (2016-2020), particularly “enhancing and diversifying sustainable
livelihoods of rural poor and marginalized groups, especially women, through
inclusive local private-sector development. Pending development of the PNSR2,
which will take place prior to preparation of the new COSOP, IFAD has developed,
together with the Government, a country strategy note covering the years 2017
and 2018. This note underscores the importance of: (i) building the resilience of

II. Project description
A. Project area and target group
5. With a view to focusing interventions to increase effectiveness and impact, while
avoiding overlap with the Participatory Natural Resource Management and Rural
Development Project in the North, Centre-North and East Regions (Neer-Tamba)
now under way, PAPFA will focus on the regions of Boucle du Mouhoun, Cascades
and Hauts-Bassins where it will build on and scale up the gains made by the
Agricultural Commodity Chain Support Project (PROFIL), the Rural Business
Services Development Project (PASPRU), Neer-Tamba and the Agricultural
Diversification and Market Development Project (PAFASP). Boucle du Mouhoun is
one of the most vulnerable regions in terms of poverty, food security and
malnutrition; Cascades and Hauts Bassins are among the moderately vulnerable
regions.
6.
7.
The PAPFA target group will essentially be composed of: (i) smallholder farmers
working in the value chains and their organizations, including both grassroots and
apex organizations; and (ii) agro-entrepreneurs operating upstream and
downstream of the value chains and their organizations, particularly those involved
in processing and marketing. The project will also support actors working in related
activities and occupations along the value chains, including input suppliers, those
selling and repairing agricultural supplies and equipment, transporters, etc. To
promote value chain structuring, the project will work with unions of grassroots
farmers associations, federations of unions and interprofessions within the targeted
value chains.
PAPFA will reach 57,000 households, of which 27,500 households will receive
production support (12,000 for rice, 8,000 for market gardening, 3,000 for sesame
and 1,500 for cowpea), 27,000 households will be supported in setting up or
consolidating rural microenterprises, and 2,500 households will be supported under
cross-cutting and structuring activities for value chains and tracks. Considering that
the average household size is six people, the project aims to reach 342,000 people.
PAPFA will endeavour to reach a minimum of 50 per cent women and 30 per cent
young people on all interventions.
B. Project development objective
8.
The overall objective of PAPFA is to contribute to poverty reduction and stimulate
economic growth in the regions of Boucle du Mouhoun, des Cascades et des Hauts-
Bassins.
9.
The development objective is to sustainably improve the food security and incomes
of farmers working in production and value addition in the rice, market gardening,
sesame and cowpea value chains.
10. The impact indicators are: (i) the number of people benefiting from economic
mobility; (ii) the percentage of households undergoing a lean period of no more
than two months; (iii) the number of beneficiaries receiving services promoted by
the project; and (iv) the percentage of households reporting an increase in their
incomes of at least 30 per cent in the targeted value chains.
C. Components/outcomes
11.
PAPFA will be implemented over a period of six years (2018-2023) and will
comprise two investment components: (i) agricultural productivity and
production (US$49.7 million or 69.3 per cent of total cost); and (ii) value addition
and marketing of agricultural products (US$12.9 million or 18 per cent of total
cost); and a management component covering coordination, monitoring and
2
EB 2017/122/R.6/Rev.1
evaluation (M&E) and knowledge management (US$9.1 million or 12.7 per cent of
total cost).
12. The expected outcome of component A is an improvement in the productivity of
agricultural systems in the targeted value chains. This outcome will be achieved by:
(i) facilitating access to quality inputs and materials, and adapted agricultural
advice; and (ii) installing hydro-agricultural improvements and storage and roads
infrastructure at production sites.
13. The expected outcome of component B is an improvement in value addition and
marketing of agricultural products in the targeted value chains. This outcome will
be achieved by: (i) strengthening value chain organizations – grassroots producers’
organizations, federations and interprofessions; and (ii) professionalizing rural
microenterprises.
III. Project implementation
A. Approach
14. Agricultural value chain approach: PAPFA will adopt the value chain approach to
develop close links between actors involved in production and post-harvest
activities – processing, storage and marketing – in order to create value.
15. Entrepreneurial approach: PAPFA will treat agriculture as a business to enable
smallholder farmers to generate a surplus. The project will facilitate commercial
partnerships, particularly contracts between farmers’ organizations and actors
downstream of value chains.
16. The project will take an outsourcing approach, relying on private and public
service providers, performance agreements with proximity operators and
arrangements with public technical services.
B. Organizational framework
17. The Ministry of Agriculture and Water (MAAH) will provide technical oversight of
PAPFA. The MAAH will set up a national steering committee in accordance with
prevailing regulations. The steering committee will provide guidance, approval of
annual workplans and budgets (AWP/Bs) and monitoring.
18.
PAPFA will have a light national project coordination unit (PCU) based in
Ouagadougou. Two regional coordination units (RCUs) located in Bobo-Dioulasso
and Dédougou will provide coordination, management and monitoring of activities
and contracts with service providers, and will manage relations with beneficiaries
and implementation partners.
C. Planning, M&E, learning and knowledge management
19.
Planning and budgeting of PAPFA activities will be done using AWP/Bs. Each draft
AWP/B will comprise: (i) a detailed description of activities planned for the coming
year, together with implementation modalities and monitoring indicators; (ii) a
funds flow statement and disbursement plan; and (iii) a procurement plan.
20. The M&E system will be built around four main pillars: (i) the logical framework,
outlining the project’s intervention logic and the chain of expected outcomes;
(ii) the AWP/B, as the tool for planning activities to achieve the objectives set;
(iii) the M&E manual, specifying the arrangements and the tools and actors to
implement them; and (iv) the information systems platform developed to meet the
project needs, to record achievements and monitor the status of activities and
progress made on achieving expected outcomes.
21. The system will be operationalized locally by the implementation partners, who will
plan and monitor activities, and collect data. Data will be compiled by PCU staff as
needed for analysis and periodic reporting.
3
EB 2017/122/R.6/Rev.1
22. The M&E system will be participatory and will include: (i) ongoing internal activity
monitoring; (ii) participatory internal self-evaluations involving all stakeholders,
including the beneficiaries; (iii) joint supervision missions by IFAD and national
authorities, and implementation support missions; (iv) periodic external
evaluations; and (v) a midterm review conducted jointly by IFAD and the national
authorities at the end of year four.
23. Learning and knowledge management. To encourage the sharing of knowledge
and experience between the two IFAD-funded projects, a knowledge management
and communication officer will be recruited to work with the project portfolio. Both
projects will pay particular attention to capitalizing gains and managing knowledge
generated throughout project implementation. For PAPFA, the M&E and knowledge
management officer will backstop the knowledge management and communication
officer for the two projects. Technical officers with the PCU and RCUs will work
closely with the capitalization process. Knowledge management and communication
matters will be guided by the knowledge management and communication strategy
to be prepared by a service provider at project start-up.
24. Communication among stakeholders to share best practices and knowledge
acquired will take place in thematic exchange groups, guided exchange visits,
national and regional workshops, and so on.
D. Financial management, procurement and governance
25. The administrative and financial management of the project, in addition to fiduciary
issues, will be based mainly on the arrangements and procedures already in use by
Neer-Tamba. The project funds will be managed in accordance with prevailing
procedures in Burkina Faso, and with IFAD’s procedures on financial management
for projects. They will be outlined in the borrower letter and in the project manual
of administrative, financial and management procedures. The Neer-Tamba manual
will serve as a basis and will be adapted as needed for PAPFA.
26.
IFAD’s procurement guidelines provide the regulatory framework for this project.
Contracts for goods, works and services funded by IFAD will be awarded in
accordance with the borrower’s procurement regulations (national procedures), to
the extent that they are compatible with the Fund’s guidelines.
E. Supervision
27.
PAPFA will be supervised directly by IFAD in collaboration with the Government of
Burkina Faso, with two supervision missions per year. The supervision missions will
pay particular attention to monitoring implementation, the achievement of results,
outcomes and impact, and the effectiveness of institutional and technical gains to
be consolidated and scaled up. Specific support missions will take place as needed.

32. Economic results and sensitivity analysis. The project reaches the break-even
point as of year five. Sixty-nine per cent of income is derived from production
activities and 31 per cent from value addition.
33. Based on these assumptions, economic NPV is about US$42.4 million. The EIRR for
the project as a whole is estimated at 18.4 per cent.
34. However, the project returns are actually higher considering the benefits accruing
from rural tracks rehabilitation and nutritional education for women and members
of cooperatives, in addition to functional literacy training, which will have a positive
impact on food and nutritional security and therefore improve the quality of life.
35. The sensitivity analysis indicates that the project is quite robust. Even with the
most pessimistic assumptions of a 30 per cent drop in income, a 50 per cent cost
increase, a four-year delay in benefits or a 20 per cent increase in production costs,
the EIRR remains above the opportunity cost of capital.
D. Sustainability
36. The sustainability of the investments and expected benefits is attributable to:
(i) sector professional organizations, based on expressed demand, together with
land tenure assurances and participation by beneficiaries and rural communities in
the investment; (ii) the selection of proven techniques and technologies that can be
managed locally; and (iii) structuring and support for farmers in managing,
maintaining and repairing the improvements made.
37. The probability that the project achievements will be maintained over the long term
is high. Financing of water-smart irrigation equipment and provision of quality
inputs and appropriate advice along the value chains will provide actors with the
means to boost the competitiveness and quality of their products. The programmed
actions are intended to make agricultural professional organizations (APOs) active
partners developing an entrepreneurial culture rather than beneficiaries.

E. Risk identification and mitigation
38. The main risks to PAPFA relate to: (i) delays in implementing the subprojects for
market access; (ii) the possibility that farmers will not take an interest in agro-
ecological technologies; (iii) major climate hazards; (iv) a poorly functioning market
for the products supported; (v) reluctance among microfinance institutions and
banks to take on medium-term risks to fund equipment for rural microenterprises;
and (vi) weak project coordination and management. Mitigation measures to
address technical risks include: (i) an innovative procedures manual with realistic
approval procedures; (ii) a gradual introduction of agro-ecological measures;
(iii) adapted hydro-agricultural improvements, improved and adapted seed and the
application of mitigation and climate change resilience measures; (iv) support for
quality production and commercial contracting; (v) support for rural
entrepreneurship resource centres (CREER) to set up financing packages and
monitor credit to lower credit risks; and (vi) ongoing capacity-building for
management teams.
V. Corporate considerations
A. Compliance with IFAD policies
39.
IFAD’s Strategic Framework 2016-2025. PAPFA is aligned with IFAD’s Strategic
Framework 2016-2025, which calls for enabling inclusive and sustainable rural
transformation. PAPFA will implement activities to underpin the achievement of
IFAD’s three strategic objectives: (i) improve poor rural people’s productive
capacities in a sustainable and resilient way; (ii) increase poor rural people’s
benefits from market participation; and (iii) strengthen the environmental
sustainability and climate resilience of poor rural people’s economic activities.
40. Country strategy note 2017-2018. PAPFA is fully aligned with IFAD’s planned
commitments in Burkina Faso as outlined in the country strategy note 2017-2018.
PAPFA is aligned with the vision set forth in the note, which calls for sustainably
increasing income and employment opportunities for rural people and building
climate change resilience by developing agricultural value chains.
41. Climate change strategy. PAPFA is aligned with IFAD’s climate change strategy
(2010), in particular with the following objectives: (i) to support innovative
approaches to help smallholder farmers build their resistance to climate change;
(ii) to help smallholder farmers take advantage of available mitigation incentives
and funding; and (iii) to inform a more coherent dialogue on climate change, rural
development, agriculture and food security.
42.
43.
44.
IFAD’s rural finance policy. The project will put forward mechanisms and
activities that are aligned with the national strategy for the microfinance sector
(2015), as well as IFAD’s strategies, principles and tools in rural finance and value
chain financing.
IFAD’s targeting strategy. The PAPFA targeting strategy is consistent with IFAD’s
targeting policy, which is based on geographic targeting, direct targeting and self-
targeting. PAPFA is committed to helping poor rural people, women and young
people – priority beneficiaries who are at the heart of IFAD’s work. The project
support has been designed in consideration of the diversified profiles of target
groups, including gender analysis to take into account sex and age, and a
livelihoods analysis to reflect income bracket.
Integrating smallholder producers and the private sector into agricultural
value chains. Aligned. The development approach adopted by PAPFA is centred
around value chains and based on an analysis of the value chain as a whole for
each of the four value chains selected, from producer to final consumer. The value
chain approach adopted takes into account all stakeholders in each value chain – in

addition to the poor rural people, young people and women comprising IFAD’s main
target group – and their interactions.
45. Social, environmental and climate assessment (SECAP). Aligned.
B. Alignment and harmonization
46.
PAPFA is aligned with the National Policy on Food and Nutritional Security (PNSAN)
in working to increase product availability, nutritional value, health safety and
nutritional quality and to promote good nutrition.
47. Burkina Faso’s agriculture sector receives technical and financial support from
several bilateral and multilateral partners. In an ongoing effort to create
complementarities with other partners in the rural development process, IFAD,
through PAPFA, will prioritize harmonization and complementarity of operations in
the field. Accordingly, given the progress made on the Samandeni Valley Integrated
Development Project in the Hauts-Bassins region, with the prospect of developing
21,000 ha for rice growing, consideration will be given to opportunities for
partnership and exchanges with this important programme. With respect to the
public-private partnership on infrastructure, the World Bank Group’s Global
Infrastructure Facility has expressed interest in collaborating with PAPFA. In
addition, there is a possible partnership with the Smallholder and Small and
Medium-sized Enterprise Investment Finance Fund (SIF), an investment fund under
preparation by IFAD’s Policy and Technical Advisory Division that will improve
access to finance for the best performing rural microenterprises once it becomes
operational. Finally, a close partnership with the African Development Bank and the
International Institute of Tropical Agriculture of the Consultative Group for
International Agricultural Research is planned on the Technologies for African
Agricultural Transformation initiative, which calls for fostering youth
entrepreneurship by promoting new technologies in the agriculture sector. IFAD will
work closely with the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization and the
World Food Programme on improving productivity and market access.
C.
48.
Innovation and scaling up
PAPFA draws on lessons learned from IFAD-funded projects, including PASPRU and
other IFAD projects in Guinea, Côte d’Ivoire and Senegal. Within the framework of
PASPRU, a partnership with the Institute for Research in Applied Science and
Technology led to producing a catalogue of technologies and innovations, guides to
good hygiene practices and manufacturing agrifood products with commercial
potential for rural microenterprises. These have been translated into local
languages and support provided for research and development on improving
processing technologies for private-sector actors and scaling up prototypes with
local artisans. These good practices will be replicated by PAPFA where relevant.
49.
In addition, innovations will be proposed in terms of agro-ecological practices to
reduce the use of synthetic commercial inputs that are hazardous to the
environment and human health and to promote ecologically sustainable practices.
50. Scaling up. PAPFA has been designed to integrate and scale up tried and tested
technical and organizational solutions, such as: (a) developing a limited number of
value chains that can create decent, green jobs for young people and women;
(b) generating extra income by supporting the development of rural
microenterprises upstream and downstream of the value chains; (c) supporting
structural investments in competitive, sustainable agricultural and market
gardening systems based on mobilization and smart use of water to build resilience
to climate change; (d) promoting technologies and inputs for agricultural small
enterprises; and (e) reducing post-harvest losses by installing storage and
marketing infrastructure and adopting conservation and processing techniques.
