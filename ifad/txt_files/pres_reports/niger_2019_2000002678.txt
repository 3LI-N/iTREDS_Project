National context
The people of the Republic of the Niger make their living mainly from small-scale
family crop farming and livestock production, which employ more than 85 per cent
of the economically active population. The structure of the gross domestic product
is relatively stable, with a predominant agriculture sector (43.4 per cent of the
gross domestic product in 2018), followed by the service sector (35 per cent) and
the industrial sector (14.9 per cent). The rural sector is subject to climate hazards,1
which adversely affect agriculture through the degradation of natural resources.
The political climate is stable. However, the country’s security is threatened by the
repeated attacks of terrorist groups in the Diffa (Lake Chad area) and Tillabéry
regions, and northern Tahoua, where a state of emergency is in effect.2
Niger’s goal of reducing poverty (Sustainable Development Goal 1) from
45.1 per cent to 37.9 per cent by 2021 (Economic and Social Development Plan3)
and to 20 per cent by 2035 (Sustainable Development and Inclusive Growth
Strategy4) is a real challenge.
Special aspects relating to IFAD’S corporate mainstreaming priorities
Gender. With a gender inequality ranking among the highest in the world (0.713),
Niger is a country with serious challenges in terms of gender equality and women’s
empowerment and participation in the development process. With a roughly
52.4 per cent female population in 2018, the average fertility rate is 7.6 children
per woman. Despite recent legislative and regulatory efforts, the inequalities
between women and men persist and are manifested in gaps in access to basic
social services, productive resources (land), education, financial services and
markets, as well as decision-making bodies. Early marriage and pregnancy limit
opportunities for women’s empowerment and girls’ access to education.
In this context, the Project to Strengthen Resilience of Rural Communities to Food
and Nutrition Insecurity (PRECIS) will take a transformative approach to the
gender issue that will go beyond improving women’s access to resources and
enable communities to explore and question the social norms that create these
inequalities.
Youth. People under the age of 15 account for 52 per cent of Niger’s population.
The country’s 3.9 per cent annual demographic growth poses a challenge for food
and nutrition security, health, education, employment, socioeconomic integration
and access to basic services.5 The problems facing youth are unresolved: lack of
jobs, both urban and rural; limited access to productive resources (land, credit)

Nutrition. The agriculture sector’s high exposure to agro-climate risks and the
high poverty rate are the main sources of vulnerability to food insecurity and
malnutrition. According to Niger’s 2017 demographic and health survey, some
42 per cent of children under 5 suffer from stunting, with the highest prevalence
found in the Maradi and Zinder regions. The prevalence of acute malnutrition or
emaciation in 2018 was 16 per cent in children aged 6 to 59 months, especially in
the Maradi, Zinder and Tahoua regions.6
Environment and climate change. With 80 per cent of its surface area in the
Sahara and Sahel and the desert expanding by 200,000 hectares per year, the
effects of climate change are manifested in Niger by: (i) water shortages;
(ii) irregular and lower rainfall and iii) higher temperatures. The most visible
impacts are: (i) an acceleration of desertification and soil erosion; (ii) more
frequent extreme climate events (drought and floods)7 and (iii) loss of
biodiversity.8
Rationale for IFAD engagement
IFAD’s country strategic opportunities programme for Niger seeks sustainable
improvement of the food security of rural households and their increased resilience.
This objective is consistent with the principal development policies of the
Government of Niger Government since 20129 and its Pro-Resilience Programme
2019-2021. It corresponds to the following three lines of action: (i) growth and
diversification of agro-sylvo-pastoral and fishery production; (ii) steady supply of
agricultural and agrifood products to rural and urban markets; (iii) improving the
resilience of populations to food crises and disasters, within the framework of the
"Nigériens nourrissent les Nigériens" Initiative. PRECIS is aimed at intensifying
IFAD interventions in the aforementioned priority areas.
9.
IFAD operations in the country involve a long-term programmatic approach, and
PRECIS capitalizes on the achievements of the Family Farming Development
Programme (ProDAF) to heighten impacts and expand them to new municipalities.
B. Lessons learned from experience
10.
PRECIS will benefit from the lessons learned from IFAD operations in Niger and
other rural projects in Africa, especially: (i) the country programme approach
followed by ProDAF since 2015, consolidating IFAD interventions under a coherent
framework that permits consensus-based planning of activities and financing to
ensure coherent operational approaches; (ii) the economic development hub
approach linking production basins to markets whose products are destined for
urban centres; (iii) improvement of the cost-sharing approach for financing
remunerative activities, especially market gardening, supported by ProDAF, which
gives small-scale producers access to bank financing; (iv) the promotion of
small-scale irrigation, which has yielded good results in the Ruwanmu Small-scale
Irrigation project; (v) the promotion of renewable energy in value chains to lower
costs and reduce greenhouse gas emissions from small-scale irrigation and
facilitate refrigeration (vaccines, food preservation) and home lighting; vi) the use

II. Project description
A. Project objectives, geographic area of intervention and target
groups
11. The project’s overall goal is to sustainably improve the food and nutrition security
of rural households and strengthen their resilience to climate and environmental
shocks. Its development objective is to increase the income of rural households,
improve their livelihoods and ensure the socioeconomic integration of young people
(both women and men) in promising rural professions.
12.
Project intervention area. The project will cover 186 municipalities in the Dosso,
Tahoua, Maradi and Zinder regions, which have 46 municipalities and
6,606 villages more than the ProDAF intervention area.
13. Target group. PRECIS will directly benefit 209,722 households, or
1,468,054 people, consisting of: (i) settled smallholders engaged in grain (maize,
sorghum, millet, rice) production, market gardening, poultry production and small
animal husbandry; (ii) young women and men (aged 18-35) with micro- and small
rural enterprises wishing to invest in these value chains; (iii) women wishing to
engage in remunerative activities; (iv) actors upstream and downstream of
production, involved in related activities (supply distribution, marketing, processing
and farm equipment maintenance) and the delivery of other services;
(v) professional organizations; (vi) vulnerable farming households;
(vii) transhumant pastoralists (Tuareg nomads) and (viii) persons with disabilities.
B. Components/outcomes and activities
14.
PRECIS will revolve around the following components: (i) sustainable agricultural
development and strengthening of rural household resilience; (ii) promotion of
youth entrepreneurship and market access and (iii) coordination, civic
engagement, monitoring and evaluation, capitalization and knowledge
management.
Component 1: Sustainable agricultural development and strengthening of
rural household resilience. This is divided into three subcomponents:
-
-
-
Subcomponent 1.1: “Improving sustainable surface water and land
management,” will mitigate the effects of desertification and climate change
through measures to restore productivity to waters in catchment areas and
promote technologies for the sustainable management of water and land
resources in production basins (land recovery, water works and the
development of production basins).
Subcomponent 1.2: “Strengthening of beneficiary technical and institutional
capacities,” will strengthen the capacity of smallholders and their
organizations to boost their production (crop and livestock) and productivity
and sustainably manage their farms; it will also build capacity among local
technical assistance providers.
Subcomponent 1.3: “Education, the promotion of good nutrition practices and
cross-cutting measures,” will help to improve food and nutrition security
through activities to prevent malnutrition (feeding, nutrition and hygiene
practices, the construction of granaries to ensure the availability of food
during the hunger season), functional literacy activities and interactive
training on gender issues and women’s leadership.

Component 2: Promotion of youth entrepreneurship and market access.
This is divided into two subcomponents:
-
-
Subcomponent 2.1: “Support to youth for the creation and development of
sustainable rural enterprises,” will, with the support of local technical
assistance services, encourage rural entrepreneurship and improve young
rural entrepreneurs’ ability to create or grow their business. PRECIS will
encourage financial institutions to develop customized products and ensure
the financial education of beneficiaries.
Subcomponent 2.2: “Rural market development,” will improve the conditions
for market access and the marketing of agropastoral products by building
marketing infrastructure (collection centres, wholesale markets) and
eliminating isolation (rehabilitation of rural roads).
Component 3: Coordination, civic engagement, monitoring and evaluation
and knowledge management. This will ensure the orientation,
management and monitoring and evaluation of the project. It includes:
-
-
Subcomponent 3.1: “Monitoring and evaluation, capitalization, gender and
social inclusion, knowledge management and communication,” will set up a
monitoring and evaluation mechanism linked with ProDAF’s existing
mechanism. The project will have dedicated human resources for
operationalizing the gender, youth and nutrition strategies.
Subcomponent 3.2: “Coordination and civic engagement,” will ensure the
project’s human resource and financial management and develop the
necessary partnerships for effective results-based implementation. PRECIS
will establish third-party monitoring mechanisms for evaluating the overall
management of the project.
C. Theory of change
15.
PRECIS activities will provide solutions for the major food and nutrition insecurity
problems facing rural households in Niger and the limited investment of young
people in agropastoral value chains. These problems include, in particular:
(i) limited water mobilization and management capacity for agropastoral production
activities, due to climate events and environmental constraints; (ii) the low level of
agricultural and pastoral production due to the accentuation and unpredictability of
climate change, heavy human and animal dependence on natural resources,
unsuitable farming practices, limited access to quality inputs (seed, fertilizers) and
production factors (land); (iii) a large population of unemployed youth resulting
from a lack of assistance tailored to the needs of youth and women;
(iv) socio-cultural barriers that create serious gender inequalities and limit
women’s access to land, capital, decision-making bodies and education;
(v) little marketing of agricultural products due to the absence of market
infrastructure (roads, organizational collection and storage centres) and low
processing levels; (vi) the alarming presence of chronic and acute malnutrition
caused by poor feeding practices.
16. These problems result in: (i) an agropastoral sector that holds little attraction for
youth, (ii) low household productivity, with recurring malnutrition and little
resilience among vulnerable groups such as women and youth and (iii) an
agropastoral sector that is not market-oriented.
17. Some of the changes to be introduced by the project are: (i) improved access to
water; (ii) higher crop and small animal productivity and yields through the
promotion of appropriate techniques, technical assistance services and the spread
of innovations; (ii) development of micro- and small rural enterprises by young
people, leading to job creation and income generation; (iv) greater leadership and
access by women to productive resources (land, capital); (v) improved market

D. Alignment, ownership and partnerships
18. National ownership. PRECIS grew out of the strong political will of the
Government and is aligned with the operational areas of the Plan of Action
2016-2020 of the Nigériens Nourrissent les Nigériens Initiative and the Pro-
Resilience Programme presented by the Government of Niger during the joint visit
by the President of IFAD, the Director General of the Food and Agriculture
Organization of the United Nations (FAO) and the Executive Director of the World
Food Programme (WFP) to Niger in August 2018.
19. Alignment with IFAD corporate priorities. PRECIS is consistent with IFAD’s
Strategic Framework 2016-2025, aimed at fostering inclusive and sustainable rural
transformation, and with the strategic lines of the Eleventh Replenishment of
IFAD's Resources (IFAD11). The proposed activities will make it possible to meet
IFAD’s three strategic objectives, namely: (i) sustainably and resiliently increase
poor rural people’s productive capacities, (ii) increase poor rural people’s benefits
from market participation and (iii) strengthen the environmental sustainability and
climate resilience of poor rural people’s economic activities. PRECIS is also aligned
with IFAD’s proposed interventions in Niger, as stated in the strategic objectives of
the country strategic opportunities programme 2013–2022.10
20. Contribution to the achievement of the Sustainable Development Goals. PRECIS
will contribute to the achievement of Sustainable Development Goals 1
(no poverty), 2 (zero hunger), 5 (gender equality and women’s empowerment) and
6 (clean water and sanitation).
21. Partnerships. The project will mobilize cofinancing from the following partners:
the OPEC Fund for International Development (OFID) and the African Development
Bank for market access and water infrastructure projects; the Global Environment
Fund (GEF) to improve soil use and restore ecosystems; the Green Climate Fund
(GCF) for adaptation and renewable energy promotion. The United Nations
Environment Programme and the World Resource Institute will support
implementation of the GEF activities. Ongoing partnerships with WFP and FAO
under ProDAF (land recovery, promotion of good agricultural practices) will be
pursued. Collaboration with State technical services, rural organizations, regional
councils and civil society will foster ownership and the sustainability of
investments.

groundnut), the EIRR ranges from 11.9 to 34 per cent.

B. Environmental and social category
34. The amount of land that is usable for agricultural production and grazing and the
fertility of the land in the project area are declining due to: (i) wind erosion
(desertification of production basins, pastureland, waterways, socioeconomic
infrastructure), (ii) brushfires and (iii) the steady encroachment of inedible
vegetation in pastureland. The Dosso region includes the “W” Region Biosphere
Reserve and a Ramsar site (a site on the list of wetland sites designated as having
international importance). PRECIS will ensure rational and equitable resource
management to prevent conflicts between riverside dwellers and wildlife.
35.
PRECIS interventions will reduce the vulnerability of farms to erosion and floods
and facilitate farmers’ access to factors of production. The capacity-building and
credit programmes will help create jobs and improve livelihoods. In the Dosso
region, the project will assist in the preparation and/or improvement of plans for
the development of protected areas (Ramsar sites) without intervening directly in
these sites. The project is classified in category B.
C. Climate risk classification
36. Niger is one of the Sahelian countries most vulnerable to climate change, as it is
exposed to desertification, recurrent droughts and decreasing rainfall. Its climate
risk is considered high.
D. Debt sustainability
37.
In its debt sustainability analysis of Niger, the International Monetary Fund judged
the risk of over-indebtedness as moderate. The foreign debt-to-export ratio and

and 39.5 per cent in terms of the present value, well below the established

IV. Implementation
A. Organizational framework
38.
Project management and coordination
PRECIS will be under the technical supervision of the Ministry of Agriculture. The
project will follow IFAD’s country programme approach in Niger and will have four
independent RPMUs (Maradi, Tahoua, Zinder and Dosso) responsible for the
technical, administrative and financial management of the activities in each region.
The national representation and technical assistance cell will ensure representation
and coherence of all interventions implemented under the country programme.
Financial management, procurement and governance
39. Financial management. Project funds will be managed pursuant to the current
procedures of Niger and IFAD, which will be detailed in the letter to the recipient
and the Project’s manual of administrative, financial and management procedures.
40. Procurement. The necessary infrastructure, materials and consulting services will
be delivered pursuant to Niger’s current procurement code, as long as its
provisions are consistent with those of IFAD. For amounts subject to public tender
procedures, the tender procedures will be followed.
41. Governance. PRECIS will apply the principles of good governance and
transparency.
B. Planning, monitoring and evaluation, knowledge management
and communication
42. Planning. The PRECIS planning system will replicate that of ProDAF. An annual
workplan and budget (AWBP) will be prepared.
43. Monitoring and evaluation. The indicators are presented in the logical
framework. The project will be supported by the monitoring and evaluation system
set up by ProDAF and will be expanded to the Dosso region.
44. Learning and knowledge management, communication. PRECIS will
capitalize on farmers’ knowledge through the identification of local, national and
international know-how in the field and on the financiers’ experiences in the
targeted domains (climate change, intensive farming, gender and nutrition).
45. Transparency and communication. The project will ensure transparent
management through beneficiary participation in its steering committee and
monitoring and evaluation. Stakeholders will be regularly informed about project
activities and outcomes.
46.
C.
47.
Innovation and scaling up
PRECIS will scale up the “economic development hub” approach, whose success
has been proven in the Dosso region and the northern band of Tahoua, Maradi and
Zinder. PRECIS will also promote an innovative approach to support rural youth
entrepreneurship through an enterprise assistance mechanism.
Implementation plans
Implementation readiness and start-up plans
PRECIS will use the ProDAF execution mechanism for immediate implementation. It
will avail itself of IFAD’s pre-financing facility for rapid project start-up and begin
implementing key start-up activities. A budget of EUR 1,536,000 has been
calculated for these activities (appendix 11 of the project concept note).

Supervision, midterm review and completion plans
IFAD will conduct two supervision and monitoring missions per year with the
participation of the Government and other financial partners. IFAD and the
Government will conduct a joint midterm review at the end of year 3. The
completion mission will be organized with all stakeholders at the end of the project.
V. Legal instruments and authority
49. A financing agreement between the Government of the Republic of the Niger and
IFAD shall constitute the legal instrument for extending the proposed financing to
the recipient for the implementation of PRECIS. A copy of the negotiated financing
agreement is attached in appendix I.
50. The Government of the Republic of the Niger is empowered under its laws to
receive financing from IFAD.

Annexe 1
Description du projet et Dispositions relatives à l’exécution
I.
Description du projet
1.
Zone  d’intervention. Le  Projet  couvrira  186  communes  dans  les  régions  de
Dosso,  Tahoua,  Maradi  et  Zinder.  Elle  est  limitée  au  Nord  par  la  Région  d’Agadez,
au Sud par le Nigeria et le Bénin, à l’Est par la Région de Diffa et à l’Ouest par la
Région de Tillabéry.
2.
Groupe  cible. Le Projet  touchera  directement  environ  209 722  ménages,  soit
approximativement  1  468  054  personnes. Plus  spécifiquement,  le  Projet  ciblera:
(i) les  petits  exploitants  agricoles sédentaires investis  dans  les  productions
céréalières  du  milieu  sahélien nigérien,  la  filière  riz  dans  la région de  Dosso,  le
maraîchage, l'aviculture et le petit élevage; (ii) les jeunes hommes et femmes (18
à  35  ans)  en  activité  ou  sans  emploi  et  désireux  de s’installer  dans les  différentes
filières retenues, porteurs de projets de micro et petites entreprises rurales; (iii) les
femmes productrices ou désireuses de mener des activités génératrices de revenus;
(iv) les  acteurs  en  amont  et  en  aval  de  la  production,  investis  dans  les  métiers
connexes  dont  la  distribution  d’intrants,  la  commercialisation,  la  transformation,
l’artisanat  et  la  fourniture  d’autres  services;  (v) les  organisations  professionnelles
(groupements  de  base,  coopératives,  unions,
fédérations);  (vi) les  ménages
agricoles vulnérables motivés et désirant accroître et développer leurs exploitations
agricoles;  et  (vii) les  personnes  handicapées  des  zones  rurales  qui  participent  ou
ont  l'intention  de  participer à des  activités  le  long  des  chaînes  de valeur
sélectionnées.
3.
Objectif  global  et  objectif  de  développement.  L'objectif  global  du  Projet  est
d'améliorer  durablement  la  sécurité  alimentaire  et  nutritionnelle  des  ménages
ruraux et de renforcer leur résilience face aux chocs climatiques et à la dégradation
des  ressources  naturelles.  Son  objectif  de  développement  est  d’accroitre  les
revenus  des  ménages  ruraux,  d'améliorer  leurs  moyens  de  subsistance  et
d’existence et assurer l'insertion socioéconomique des jeunes (hommes et femmes)
dans les métiers ruraux porteurs.
4.
Composantes  du  projet.  Le  Projet  appuiera  les  petits  producteurs  pour  lever
les  contraintes  de  production  et  de  commercialisation  à  travers  deux  composantes
techniques et une composante de gestion et coordination.
Composante  1 - Développement  agricole  durable  et  renforcement  de  la  résilience
des ménages ruraux
La composante 1 visera à: a) mettre en place les investissements nécessaires à la
gestion de l'eau pour les activités agricoles et  pastorales et pour la consommation
humaine, b) sécuriser  le  capital  naturel  pour  la  production  agricole  de  base;
c) assurer  l'accès  des  producteurs  aux  principaux  facteurs  et  technologies  de
production adaptées à leur environnement; d) renforcer les capacités techniques et
institutionnelles  des  acteurs  pour  assurer  leurs  métiers  dans  les  segments  des
chaines  de  valeur  retenues  et  gérer  durablement les investissements  mis  en  place
avec  l'appui  du  projet,  et  e) promouvoir  une  alimentation  diversifiée  et  de  bonnes
habitudes alimentaires pour réduire la malnutrition au sein des ménages.

dégradées (PAM), l'introduction des bonnes pratiques agricoles (FAO), la promotion
du genre et la nutrition (FAO, PAM et UNICEF).
