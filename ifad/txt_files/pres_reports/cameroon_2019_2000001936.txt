A.  National context and rationale for IFAD engagement

National context 
In 2016, Cameroon had a population of 23.7 million,1 with an annual GDP per 
capita of US$1,447 in 2017.2 Despite a slowdown in growth, which declined from 
4.4 per cent in 2016 to 3.7 per cent in 2017,3 the country has one of the most 
diversified economies in the Central Africa Economic and Monetary Community 
(CEMAC). Since 2014, Cameroon has been in the throes of a socio-economic and 
political crisis (Northwest and Southwest regions), as well as a security crisis 
(North and Far North Regions). Added to this are the perverse effects of climate 
change and increased food insecurity in the north.  
Between 2001 and 2014, overall poverty declined from 40 per cent to 
37.5 per cent, falling from 14 per cent to 9 per cent in cities but rising from 52 to 
56.8 per cent in rural areas. The three northern regions had the highest poverty 
rates in 2014: Far North (74 per cent), North (67 per cent) and Northwest 
(55 per cent). 
The agriculture sector is vitally important to the country, employing around 
50 per cent of the economically active population, with an estimated contribution to 
GDP of 22.3 per cent in 2015. Dominated by small family farms (some 2 million), 
Cameroonian agriculture is handicapped by low competitiveness, weak 
infrastructure, inadequate development of value chains and deficient planning and 
coordination capacity.  
The key national strategies for the agriculture sector are: the National Agricultural 
Investment Plan (PNIA), the Rural Sector Development Strategy, the Growth and 
Employment Strategy and Vision 2035, which has four priorities: (i) boosting 
competitiveness; (ii) developing land and water resources, improving farmers’ 
quality of life and access to suitable equipment, markets and financing; 
(iii) promoting value chains, taking environmental preservation and climate change 
into account; (iv) improving sector governance. 
Special aspects relating to IFAD's corporate mainstreaming priorities  
5.  Nutrition. The chronic malnutrition rate stands at 33 per cent nationally, with 
38 per cent of it among rural children under 5, while stunting has reached alarming 
levels: 30.6 per cent in the North and 35.9 per cent in the Far North (SMART 
2018). Project investments will be geared to reducing malnutrition in the 
intervention areas. 
6. 
Climate change. Cameroon is suffering from the pernicious effects of climate 
change, especially in the northern region, where extreme weather events are more 
frequent. Higher rainfall is expected in the country’s rainy areas (the South) and 
lower rainfall elsewhere, accompanied by higher temperatures throughout the 
territory. Activities to improve climate resilience are anticipated.  
7.  Gender. In 1999, a policy and plan to reduce gender inequalities were introduced. 
Despite some important progress, limited embrace of the gender approach by 
society is still observed, along with the persistence of cultural attitudes prejudicial

8. 
Youth. According to the 2010 population and housing census, 78 per cent of the

10. 
Rationale for IFAD involvement  
IFAD engagement in Cameroon has consisted of 11 development operations since 
1981. The Country Strategy and Programme Evaluation for Cameroon 
(CSPE 2007-2017), conducted by IFAD’s Independent Office of Evaluation in 2017, 
confirmed the importance of IFAD support and recommended the consolidation of 
PADFA’s significant achievements through a second phase.  
11.  The pursuit of IFAD engagement is also justified by the strategic importance of two 
value chains, rice and onion, to the Cameroonian economy, the opportunities for 
integrating smallholders and entrepreneurs in the value chains and the 
Government’s desire to reduce imports, which tripled from 2007 to 2017 and 
account for more than one third of the country’s trade deficit.  
12.  The PNIA aims to boost total rice production from the 2013 figure of 163,000 tons 
to 750,000 tons in 2020, which will require a major technological leap (improved 
production, post-harvest management, marketing, organization and resilience of 
farmers). Current onion production (345,000 tons in 2013, basically in the North 
and Far North) does not meet national needs and is subject to constraints related 
to storage and access to both water and quality inputs. PADFA I was focused on 
removing input, technical assistance and storage constraints, but access to water is 
still a challenge.  
13. 
IFAD technical and financial assistance for a second phase of the project will be 
aimed at consolidating and scaling up PADFA achievements and pursuing activities 
that benefit cooperatives to create the conditions for sustainable development of 
the target value chains. 
B.  Lessons learned  
14.  The first phase of the project has yielded convincing results:  
(i) 1,305 farmers’ organizations (FO) supported, exceeding the target of 1,190;  
(ii) the introduction of improved seeds on 100 per cent of the land devoted to rice 
production and 83 per cent of the land devoted to onion production; (iii) an 
increase in yields from 3.5 tons/ha to 6 tons/ha (rice) and from 12 tons/ha to 18 
tons/ha (onions) as a result of access to quality plant material and better mastery 
of technologies. The evaluation of PADFA’s impact at project completion showed 
that the average income of rice producers had increased by 70 per cent and that of 
onion producers, by 60 per cent. The main lessons from the first phase of PADFA 
are:  
(i)  Delays in entry into force and start-up activities hindered achievement of the 
project’s development objectives. Financing start-up activities through IFAD’s 
2 
EB 2019/128/R.31/Rev.1 
recently created fast implementation of project start-up (FIPS) instruments 
will accelerate the launch of the second phase;  
(ii)  Prudent investment planning and project phasing are essential for ensuring 
enough time to consolidate gains and the disengagement of the project;  
(iii)  The amount of support for cooperatives should not be uniform but rather, 
consider regional characteristics and the cooperatives’ production capacity 
and actual needs;  
(iv)  Strengthening actor capacities is essential. To guarantee genuine project 
“ownership”, this requires assistance for the duration of the project, based on 
the specific needs of farmers and their organizations;  
(v)  Limited implementation capacity of service providers has been a real 
impediment to good implementation of programme activities in the stipulated 
timeframes. Implementation on force account or direct contracting with 
companies known for their professionalism and competencies could be 
envisaged. 
II.  Project description  
A.  Objectives, geographical area of intervention and target 
groups 
Objectives 
15.  Overarching goal: Contribute to poverty reduction and improved food and nutrition 
security for the target populations.  
16.  Development objective: Sustainably increase the income and resilience of family 
farms that grow rice and onions in the project areas.  
17.  Expected outcomes:  
(i) 
(ii) 
Increased productivity and production (SO 1 of IFAD): construction of 
hydro-agricultural infrastructure and rural roads; introduction of a seed 
programme; support for access to inputs and farm equipment; 
Improved conservation, processing, and marketing of production (SO 2 of 
IFAD): construction of product storage and processing infrastructure; 
procurement of processing equipment; definition of standards; development 
of a market information system; 
(iii)  Improved resilience, nutrition, and skills (SO 3 of IFAD): organization of 
value chain actors; strengthening of farmers’ technical skills and activities to 
increase climate resilience; improvement of household nutritional status.  
Intervention area  
18.  PADFA II will be implemented in the four PADFA regions – that is, the North, Far 
North, Northwest, and West. It will operate in new villages in the 15 departments 
(11 previous ones and 4 new ones) selected on the basis of specific targeting 
criteria determined with the Government.  
Target groups 
19.  The priority targets are poor smallholders organized in 111 cooperatives and 
microentrepreneurs operating upstream and downstream in the onion and rice 
value chains (seed producers, processors, merchants, input suppliers and 
agricultural service providers). The project will reach an estimated 
31,902 households, or 216,642 people, 50 per cent of whom will be women and 
30 per cent youth.  
Targeting strategy 
20.  The strategy provides for direct targeting and self-targeting through inclusive 
transparent mechanisms that prioritize the most vulnerable social groups (women, 
3 
EB 2019/128/R.31/Rev.1 
youth) and marginalized people (persons with disabilities). They include: (i) an 
active information campaign to raise awareness among beneficiaries; (ii) an 
institutional diagnostic assessment of FOs and their classification by development 
potential; (iii) profiling of FO households by their degree of vulnerability; and (iv) a 
mechanism to mitigate the risk of elite capture. Project authorities will develop an 
operational plan. In addition to pre-established quotas for the participation of 
women and youth (50 per cent and 30 per cent, respectively), the project will help 
to transform the gender-based power dynamic, which is a structural impediment to 
women’s inclusion and empowerment.  
B.  Components, outcomes and activities 
21.  The project will have two technical components (Support for production and 
Support for marketing, the organization of value chains and access to a diversified 
diet) and one management component (Planning, monitoring and evaluation, 
knowledge management and policy dialogue).  
22.  Component 1: Support for production (US$31.6 million, or 52.7 per cent of the 
total cost). Its aim is to sustainably increase production, productivity, and climate 
resilience by (i) developing and opening production basins and (ii) intensifying 
production. 
23.  Hydro-agricultural development will consist of developing 2,570 ha of lowlands and 
plains to give them access to water and improve the production base of the rice 
and onion value chains. The developed sites are small (20-100 ha), non-contiguous 
and easily manageable modules.  
24.  The opening of production basins will consist of rehabilitating 25 km of farm roads 
in the developed sites to improve access to production sites and markets.  
25. 
Intensification of production: This will involve the creation of a sustainable 
mechanism for boosting the productivity and production of the smallholders of 111 
cooperatives: (i) development of a rice and onion seed programme to furnish 
producers with quality, high-yield, climate-resilient seeds; (ii) facilitation of their 
access to technology, equipment and inputs, in partnership with microfinance 
institutions; and (iii) strengthening the capacities of farmer field schools. 
26.  Cofinancing from the Global Environment Fund (GEF) will further the dissemination 
of good environmental practices by raising population awareness and training 
technical assistance teams in areas related to the environment and climate change.  
27.  Component 2: Support for marketing, the organization of value chains and 
access to a diversified diet (US$20.1 million, or 33.5 per cent of the total cost). 
This will involve boosting product competitiveness through better knowledge of the 
market and value addition, including local improvement of products with high 
nutritional value. The project will be implemented through four subcomponents: 
(i) improvement of product storage and processing; (ii) improvement of knowledge 
and market access; (iii) organization of value chain actors and strengthening of 
their capacities; and (iv) improvement of nutritional status.  
28. 
In the area of product storage and processing, the project will support: the 
construction of 26 rice warehouses; the introduction of two parboiling units for rice 
to benefit women from the cooperatives; the construction of 28 bioclimatic 
buildings for onion storage and five onion processing units managed by women; 
facilitation of commercial transactions through the installation of five onion 
collection and packaging centres in partnership with mayors’ offices; the promotion 
of youth employment for the distribution of farm products and inputs and the 
delivery of services (transport, maintenance, mechanisation, etc.).  
29. 
In the area of market knowledge and access, the project will facilitate access to 
market and pricing information and the definition of quality standards in 
partnership with the National Standards Agency, product advertising and the 
4 
EB 2019/128/R.31/Rev.1 
construction of supply chains with the private sector. A simplified market 
information system will be set up in partnership with the telephone companies. A 
rapid market study will enable cooperatives to develop their marketing strategies.  
30.  To organize and strengthen the capacities of value chain actors, an institutional 
analysis of PADFA’s beneficiary cooperatives will be conducted and their 
development plans updated. Assistance to new cooperatives will involve the 
formalization of their legal status and training for cooperative members, elected 
officers, and members of specialized committees. The establishment of apex 
organizations and interprofessions that was begun will be pursued.  
31.  With regard to nutrition, the project will support diversification of the diets of 
women and children under 5 through: (i) the promotion and use of garden produce 
in food rations; (ii) training in innovative food technologies for products with high 
nutritional value; (iii) promotion of nutrition sensitivity; (iv) the creation of food 
processing (nutritransfo) centres; and (v) the development and dissemination of 
nutrition communication tools. 
32.  Component 3: Planning, monitoring and evaluation, knowledge 
management, policy dialogue (US$8.2 million and 13.7 per cent of the cost 
shared by the Project Coordination and Management Unit [PCMU], 12.3 per cent, 
and the Monitoring and Evaluation [M&E] Unit, 1.4 per cent). Pursuant to the 
country strategic opportunities programme (COSOP) 2019–2024, this component 
will ensure effective project implementation by establishing a robust results-based 
management system. The project will support reforms aimed at removing the 
principal bottlenecks to value chain development, particularly those related to seed 
production and private-sector participation in rural wealth and job creation. To 
improve governance, the project will capitalize on recent IFAD initiatives in this 
area (Delivery Unit, Advancing Knowledge for Agricultural Impact Initiative 
[AVANTI], M&E Training and Certification Framework in Rural Development 
Programme [PRiME]), transparency and citizen engagement to strengthen 
MINADER capacity to improve coordination in the rural sector and the use of 
results-based management. 
C.  Theory of change 
33.  Rural populations that invest in the rice and onion value chains have three major 
problems: 
(i) 
low farm productivity, primarily due to lack of quality seeds and inputs, poor 
mastery of techniques, poor water management, the pernicious effects of 
climate change and the isolation of production basins.  
(ii)  post-harvest losses due to lack of adequate facilities for product 
storage/preservation, limited mastery of processing techniques and 
inadequate equipment, resulting in low volumes of production for the market 
and consumption;  
(iii)  marketing problems associated with the lack of market and pricing 
information, farmers’ organizations and mastery of quality standards and 
marketing techniques, creating a huge gap between farmers’ efforts and 
profits, which remain limited. 
34.  The interventions proposed for removing these constraints are organized around: 
(i) higher productivity, the construction of hydro-agricultural projects and 
rehabilitation of rural roads, the implementation of a seed programme, support for 
access to farm inputs and equipment, the strengthening of farmer capacities and 
activities to promote climate resilience; (ii) the construction of product storage and 
processing infrastructure, support with respect to processing equipment, the 
strengthening of actor capacities, nutrition education activities; (iii) action to 
organize farmers and other actors in the relevant value chains, support for bringing 
products up to standard, the development of relations with microcredit institutions 
5 
EB 2019/128/R.31/Rev.1 
and the creation of an operational market information system together with 
capacity-building.  
35.  Given the importance of reducing gender inequalities, the interventions will also 
boost the empowerment, economic clout and participation of women and youth in 
decision-making.  
36.  These interventions and the policy dialogue should have a positive impact on the 
productivity and production of the target value chains and lead to a sustainable 
increase in farmers’ income and an improvement in the food and nutrition security 
of the project’s beneficiary populations.  
D.  Alignment, ownership and partnerships 
37.  PADFA II reflects the political will to reduce rice and onion imports. It is aligned 
with the national priorities spelled out by the Government in the Rural Sector 
Development Strategy, the PNIA, the Growth and Employment Strategy and Vision 
2035. It is also consistent with IFAD’s strategic objectives and will contribute to the 
achievement of the Sustainable Development Goals, particularly SDG 1 (No 
poverty) and SDG 2 (Zero hunger). 
38.  With regard to partnerships, collaboration will be established with (i) FAO, for 
implementation of the seed component; (ii) the World Food Programme, for the 
establishment of an institutional procurement program and the strengthening of 
cooperatives in terms of quality standards; and (iii) the United Nations Children’s 
Fund, for nutrition education. The partnership with FO apex organizations, 
launched in 2017, will continue for the organization of value chains.

US$6.6 million, or 11 per cent of the total cost.

organization of value chains and access to a diversified diet", are somewhat 
relevant to climate action. According to the multilateral development banks’ joint 
methodology for tracking climate change adaptation finance and mitigation of the 
effects of climate change, the total amount allocated to the project by IFAD to 
finance climate action is estimated at US$16.66 million.

Financing and cofinancing strategy and plan 
41.  The financing plan is as follows: (i) IFAD, US$47 million; (ii) Government, around 
US$8.7 million; (iii) GEF, US$2 million, (iv) FAO, US$0.4 million; (v) beneficiaries, 
US$2.1 million. The Government contribution will cover the cost of duties and taxes 
on the goods and services procured by the project, as well as certain investments. 
Prefinancing for start-up activities (FIPS) in the amount of US$1.1 million has been 
extended to the Government to ensure rapid start-up.  
Disbursement 
42.  The Caisse autonome d’amortissement (CAA) (debt amortization fund) will open a 
designated account in local currency, depending on the type of advance. Once the 
conditions for the first disbursement have been met, IFAD, at the CAA’s request, 
will make the respective deposit in the authorized amount (FCFA 1.2 billion). The 
CAA will also open a counterpart account for receipt of the counterpart funds to be 
mobilized under the Borrower’s budgetary procedures and laws. Two operating 
accounts corresponding to the designated account and the counterpart account will 
also be opened. The PCMU will be the account holder for both. The Coordinator and 
Administrative and Finance Officer will be co-signers.  
Summary benefit and economic analysis  
43.  The project’s economic internal rate of return is estimated at 16.35 per cent, a 
percentage significantly higher than that of the opportunity cost of capital 
(12 per cent). The net present value is positive (US$7.9 million), and the cost-
benefit ratio is satisfactory (1.25). These indicators demonstrate the profitability of 
PADFA II.  
44.  The sensitivity analysis suggests low sensitivity of project profitability to 
cost-benefit variations. The project will not be more profitable with a decrease in 
benefits or an increase in costs beyond 30 per cent (economic internal rate of 
return falling to 9.5 per cent and 11.2 per cent, respectively), while a two-year 
delay in the generation of benefits will lower this rate to 11.2 per cent.  
Exit strategy and sustainability  
45.  The exit strategy designed to ensure the sustainability of the benefits and services 
introduced by the project will consist of three phases: (i) the provision of tools and 
selection of production basins, development sites and FOs/other actors (year 1); 
(ii) investments (years 2 to 4) and strengthening of the capacity of actors (FOs, 
businesses, cooperatives, apex organizations, etc.); (iii) consolidation of gains and 
disengagement in years 5 and 6. In the last two years, the team will foster:  
(i) actor ownership of the infrastructure and equipment, as well as the good 
operation of the management mechanisms introduced (management committees 
and various technical committees established in the cooperatives); (ii) the 
deconcentrated services’ responsibility for monitoring the activities, including in the 
post-project phase.

B.  Environmental and social category 
47.  The project is classified as a category “B” project, in keeping with the requirements 
of Cameroon’s current environmental, social and climate evaluation procedures, 
especially implementation of the environmental, social and climate management 
framework developed.  
C.  Climate risk classification 
48.  The climate risk has been evaluated and is considered high. Regular monitoring of 
compliance with standards and rigorous implementation of the environmental, 
social and climate management framework are provided for.  
D.  Debt sustainability  
49.  While Cameroon’s public debt has been sustainable up to this point, the risk of 
over-indebtedness remains high. Its estimated public debt was around 
32.6 per cent of GDP at the end of September 2018. The Government prioritizes 
concessional loans and limits non-concessional debt to priority projects for which no 
concessional financing is available. The Financial Law bill set the ceiling for 
non-concessional debt in 2019 at FCFA 500 billion. The Government wishes to 
contract concessional debt in the amount of FCFA 150 billion.

IV.  Implementation 
A.  Organizational framework 
50.  Supervision and piloting. PADFA II will be overseen by MINADER. A steering 
committee will be responsible for guidance and piloting. A PCMU with managerial, 
administrative and financial independence and two branch offices in each of the two 
major intervention areas have been installed in Yaoundé.  
51.  Budgeting, internal control system, principles, accounting procedures and 
reporting. Budget preparation and approval will follow the procedures detailed in 
the financial manual, using accounting software approved by IFAD. The financial 
manual will indicate all the activities necessary to meet the internal control 
objectives. Project accounts will be kept on the basis of accrual accounting, using 
the SYSCOHADA accounting system and adhering to the International Accounting 
Standards. 
52.  Audits. Internal audits will be performed regularly, based on the commitments in 
the agreement signed by the Government of Cameroon and IFAD in February 2018 
on the creation of in internal audit function overseen by an auditor and an audit 
committee from IFAD’s country program. Annual audits will be performed by a 
private auditing firm in a manner consistent with IFAD auditing directives.  
53.  Procurement. The Public Procurement Code of June 2018 gives primacy to IFAD 
procurement procedures in cases of incompatibility with national procedures. Since 
the code is not yet in force, procurement will follow IFAD procedures. 
B.  Planning, monitoring and evaluation, learning, knowledge 
management and communication  
Planning.  
54.  Project implementation will be based on the Annual Workplan and Budget, divided 
into quarterly tranches to facilitate its operationalization.  
Monitoring and evaluation.  
55.  PADFA II will opt for strong action from the outset. Monitoring and evaluation will 
have the following objectives: (i) meet project performance needs in this area; (ii) 
improve MINADER’s sectoral monitoring; (iii) promote transparency and civic 
engagement through inclusive monitoring, enabling beneficiaries to evaluate 
performance and implementation.  
Innovation and scaling up.  
56.  The project’s main innovations will be: (i) the development of small-scale ditch 
irrigation managed by smallholders, which was introduced in Cameroon by PADFA; 
(ii) the introduction of improved varieties of rice (NERICA) and onions; (iii) the 
introduction of double rice-cropping after 50 years of single-cropping; (iv) the 
production of certified rice and onion seed by local seed-farmer cooperatives; 
(v) the construction of multi-use warehouses managed by cooperatives for rice and 
onion storage and processing, taking climate change into account.  
C.  Implementation arrangements 
57.  The Government will take advantage of FIPS, IFAD’s instrument for fast 
implementation of project start-up. Supervision will be the responsibility of IFAD, 
through its country office, and the national authorities. A mid-term review will be 
conducted in year 3 to evaluate outcomes and the likelihood of meeting the 
objectives. A project completion review will complete the evaluations and provide 
objective elements for evaluating project outcomes and lessons learned that can 
inform future operations that address the same matters.

Sous-composante 1.2 - Intensification de la production (14,2% des coûts du Projet). 
Cette sous-composante vise la mise en place d’un mécanisme durable de développement 
des  filières  riz  (irrigué  et  pluvial)  et  oignon  en  vue  d’accroître  la  productivité  et,  par 
ricochet, de booster la production des petits producteurs regroupés en coopératives, sur 
un total de 23 626 ha sur la durée du PADFA II (4 226 ha d’oignon et 19 400 ha de riz).

Les actions de la sous-composante 1.2 seront organisées en trois volets: 
Volet 1.2.1. Appui au développement du programme semencier riz et oignon - en 
vue  de  faciliter  l’accès  des  bénéficiaires  aux  semences  de  qualité  et  améliorer  la 
productivité et la production d’oignon et de  riz dans la zone d’intervention, un dispositif 
d’approvisionnement en semence, partant de la multiplication de la semence de base à la 
production  de  semences  certifiées  de  riz  et  d’oignon,  sera  instauré.  Des  actions 
complémentaires  et  transversales  seront  par  ailleurs  mises  en  œuvre  dans  le  cadre  du 
financement  FEM  afin  de  renforcer  la  résilience  des  activités  (sensibilisation  des 
populations, identification de techniques durables des systèmes de cultures, etc.). 
Volet  1.2.2.  Appui  à  l’accès  aux  intrants  et  aux  équipements  agricoles  –  vise  à 
faciliter  l’acquisition  par  les  producteurs  d’intrants  et  équipements  à  travers  un 
financement  en  fonds  de  roulement  (FDR)  unique  (une  moyenne  de  2,5  millions 
FCFA/coopérative  semencière  et  5  millions  FCFA/coopérative  de  production)  pour  la 
première campagne agricole.  
Volet  1.2.3.  Appui au renforcement des capacités techniques des  producteurs - 
vise,  à  travers  des  actions  organisées  autour  des  parcelles  de  démonstration,  des 
champs-écoles des producteurs, la planification et le suivi des activités engagées. 
Composante 2: Appui à la commercialisation, à la structuration des filières et à 
l’accès à la diversité alimentaire. Elle vise l’amélioration de la compétitivité des produits 
à  travers  une  meilleure  connaissance  et  pénétration  du  marché  et  la  valorisation  des 
produits y compris la valorisation locale de produits à haute qualité nutritive. La mise en 
œuvre de cette composante se fera à travers quatre sous-composantes: i) l’amélioration 
du stockage et de la transformation des produits; ii) l’amélioration de la connaissance et 
de l’accès au marché; iii) la structuration et le renforcement des capacités des acteurs des 
filières; iv) l’amélioration de la situation nutritionnelle.  
La composante 2 est articulée autour des quatre sous-composantes suivantes:  
Sous-composante  2.1  -  Amélioration  du  stockage  et  de  la  transformation  des 
produits. Les actions de cette sous-composante 2.1 seront organisées en deux volets: 
Volet 2.1.1. Infrastructures et équipements de transformation éprouvés - vise une 
différenciation des investissements en la matière, en fonction des coopératives, sur la base 
d’un  processus  d’élaboration  et  d’un  plan  d’affaires.  Dans  ce  cadre,  le  Projet  financera 
notamment  des  études  diagnostiques,  travaux  de  mise  aux  normes,  unités  d’étuvage, 
construction d’unité de production et de conservation, etc. 
Volet  2.1.2.  Amélioration  de  la  qualité  -  a  pour  objectif  de  permettre  une  prise  de 
conscience des enjeux liés à la qualité de la production des filières ciblées à travers la mise 
en  place  d’une  série  de  mesures  d’accompagnement  des  acteurs  des  différents  maillons 
des  filières  ciblées  telles  que  i)  le  renforcement  des  compétences  techniques, 
organisationnelles et de gestion des acteurs directs; ii) la mise à disposition de normes, de 
manuels d’utilisation, d’entretien et de gestion des équipements et des infrastructures; iii) 
le renforcement des capacités de services d’appui de proximité à la mécanisation; et enfin 
iv) la facilitation de l’accès aux informations sur les technologies.  
Sous-composante 2.2 - Amélioration de la connaissance et de l’accès au marché 
(0,8%  des  coûts  totaux  du  Projet).  Cette  sous-composante  vise  une  meilleure 
connaissance  des  marchés  des  produits  issus  des  filières  riz  et  oignon,  pour  le 
développement de stratégies de commercialisation adaptées pour une meilleure inclusion 
des petits producteurs regroupés au sein des coopératives.

Appendice I

Sous-composante 2.3 - Structuration et renforcement des capacités des acteurs 
des filières. Cette sous-composante vise l’amélioration de l’organisation des acteurs des 
filières  (coopératives,  les  interprofessions,  et  les  opérateurs  privés)  notamment  par 
l’incitation à la collaboration entre les différents maillons. 
Les actions de la sous-composante 2.3 seront organisées en deux volets: 
Volet 2.3.1. Structuration et renforcement de capacités des coopératives – a pour 
objectif la mise en œuvre d’actions d’appui différenciées définies en fonction du niveau de 
maturité des coopératives bénéficiaires.  
Volet 2.3.2. Structuration et renforcement des capacités des autres acteurs des 
filières  -  vise  à  impulser  une  dynamique  dans  le  développement  des  filières  ainsi  que 
l’émergence  d’associations  professionnelles  dans  les  autres  maillons  avec  l’aide  des 
services de l’État. 
Sous-composante 2.4 - Amélioration de la situation nutritionnelle (0,5% des coûts 
totaux). Cette sous-composante vise à contribuer, de manière ciblée, à la diminution du 
taux  de  malnutrition  chronique  chez  l’enfant  de  moins  de  cinq  ans  en  favorisant,  une 
réduction i) des carences et ii) de l’insuffisance pondérale à travers la mise en œuvre des 
mesures  suivantes: diversification de la production, transformation et commercialisation 
locale de compléments alimentaires nécessaires au développement psychomoteur du jeune 
enfant  à  travers  les  "centres  nutritransfo"  catalyseurs  de  la  transformation,  et  le 
renforcement des connaissances et pratiques alimentaires. 
Composante  3:  Planification,  Suivi-Évaluation,  Gestion  des  connaissances  et 
Communication et dialogue sur les politiques. Cette composante vise à assurer une 
exécution efficace du Projet à travers la mise en place d’un système robuste de gestion et 
communication  des  résultats.  Le  Projet  appuiera  les  réformes  visant  à  supprimer  les 
principaux goulots d’étranglements au développement des chaînes de valeur agricoles, en 
particulier  les  réformes  liées  aux  semences,  et  à  la  participation  du  secteur  privé.  Une 
étude de politique sera menée pour faire l’état des lieux des réformes en cours, et définir 
les mesures incitatives susceptibles de permettre  au secteur privé de jouer un rôle plus 
important dans la création de richesse et d’emplois en zones rurales. En ce qui concerne 
la  gouvernance,  le  Projet  tirera  parti  des  initiatives  Delivery  Unit,  AVANTI,  PRiME, 
transparence  et  engagement  citoyen,  pour  renforcer  les  capacités  du  Ministère  de 
l’agriculture, en vue d’améliorer la coordination du secteur rural, et l’utilisation de la gestion 
axée sur les résultats en tant qu’approche globale pour l’excellence.

11.  Suivi  et  évaluation.  Une  revue  à  mi-parcours  sera  organisée  durant  la  troisième

qui pourront bénéficier aux futures opérations sur les mêmes thématiques.

administratives  acceptable  pour  le  FIDA  sera  préparé  qui  inclura,  notamment:  i)  la

financière et de rapports; iii) les procédures de recrutement du personnel clé du Projet ou 
de toute assistance technique; iv) une description détaillée des modalités de mise en œuvre 
du  Projet;  et  v)  toutes  autres  procédures  ou  modalités  administratives,  financières  ou 
techniques requises par le Projet. 
13.  Approbation  et  Adoption.  Ce  projet  de  Manuel  sera  adressé  au  FIDA  pour 
commentaire et approbation, et sera adopté par l’Emprunteur tel qu’approuvé par le FIDA. 
Une  copie  sera  communiquée  au  FIDA  dans  les  meilleurs  délais.  L’Emprunteur  devra 
exécuter le Projet conformément au Manuel et ne pourra le modifier, l’abroger, y déroger 
ou permettre de le modifier, de l’abroger ou d’y déroger sans l’accord écrit préalable du 
FIDA.
